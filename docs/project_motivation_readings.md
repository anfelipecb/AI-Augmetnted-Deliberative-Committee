# Project Motivation Readings

Here is a curated list of readings from the course syllabus, reading summaries, and memos that can effectively motivate the use of AI agents, digital doubles, and societies of thought in your project.

### 1. Kim, Lai, Scherrer, Agüera y Arcas, & Evans (2026). *Reasoning Models Generate Societies of Thought.*
- **Location:** Week 7 Readings Summary (`assignments/memow7/readings_summary_w7.md`)
- **Relevance:** This is the core paper to motivate your jury deliberation. It demonstrates that models achieve superior reasoning by internally simulating multi-agent dialogue—a "society of thought." This directly grounds your mechanism of having diverse experts debate and reconcile conflicting views.

### 2. Broska et al. (2025). *The Mixed Subjects Design: Treating Large Language Models as Potentially Informative Observations.*
- **Location:** Week 4 Readings Summary (`assignments/readings/w4/w4_readings_summary.md`)
- **Relevance:** This paper formally introduces using "Silicon Subjects" alongside human data. It motivates your use of "digital doubles" for the community stakeholders by addressing the massive costs and logistical difficulties of recruiting geographically and demographically specific human subjects for policy evaluation.

### 3. Chen et al. (2025). *Persona Vectors: Monitoring and Controlling Character Traits in Language Models.*
- **Location:** Week 6 Readings Summary (`assignments/memow6/summary_readings.md`)
- **Relevance:** Motivates the technical foundation of your distinct personas (Sarah Chen, Marcus Thompson, etc.). It proves that targeted traits and analytical lenses (e.g., fiscal focus, equity focus) can be reliably extracted and controlled, making your expert jury methodologically sound.

### 4. Lai et al. (2025). *Biased AI improves human decision-making but reduces trust.*
- **Location:** Week 4 Readings Summary (`assignments/readings/w4/w4_readings_summary.md`)
- **Relevance:** It shows that "biased" or opinionated agents actually improve decision-making tasks by breaking neutral ambiguity. This perfectly justifies giving each of your jury members a strict, specific "bias" or lens (e.g., exclusively caring about fiscal responsibility).

### 5. Gabriel, Keeling, Manzini, & Evans (2025). *We need a new ethics for a world of AI agents.*
- **Location:** Week 9 Memo (`assignments/memow9/memo_w9.md`)
- **Relevance:** This can be used to motivate the project's emphasis on transparency. By keeping logs of the deliberation and surfacing dissenting opinions rather than a black-box answer, you align with the need for new ethical frameworks for autonomous agents evaluating high-stakes urban policy.
