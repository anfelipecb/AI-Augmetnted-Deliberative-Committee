# Sample Chicago Stadium Policy Proposal

## Executive Summary

This proposal outlines a policy framework for the City of Chicago's support of professional sports teams and facilities. It addresses the Harris Public Policy Challenge question: *What should be the City of Chicago's policy toward supporting professional sports teams and facilities?*

## Context

Chicago is home to the Bears (NFL), White Sox and Cubs (MLB), Bulls and Sky (NBA/WNBA), Blackhawks (NHL), and Fire (MLS). Stadiums and arenas are woven into the civic and economic fabric of the city, generating tax revenue, catalyzing development, and fostering civic identity. Current debates center on equity, displacement, fiscal impact, and long-term value as the Bears consider relocation and the Sox explore a new South Side ballpark.

## Policy Framework

### Public Funding and Subsidies

The proposal recommends a tiered approach to public investment:

- **Infrastructure-only model**: The City invests in transit, utilities, and surrounding public infrastructure (e.g., CTA extensions, pedestrian plazas) rather than direct stadium construction subsidies. Projected infrastructure investment: $200–400M for a major facility, with clawback provisions if the team relocates within 20 years.

- **Revenue-sharing**: Any new facility would be subject to a 2% surcharge on ticket sales above a baseline, with 50% directed to a Community Benefits Fund and 50% to general revenue.

- **Accountability**: Transparent financial agreements, third-party audits, and sunset clauses (e.g., 25-year term) to limit long-term fiscal exposure.

### Community Benefits and Equity

- **Affordable access**: Require 5% of tickets per event at below-market rates for Chicago residents in qualifying zip codes; partnerships with youth leagues and local organizations for free or subsidized attendance.

- **Community Benefits Agreement (CBA)**: Legally enforceable CBA with surrounding neighborhoods covering jobs (local hiring targets), anti-displacement measures, and small business support.

- **Mixed-use development**: Stadiums designed for year-round use—concerts, conventions, community events—to maximize public benefit beyond game days.

### Sustainability

- **Environmental standards**: LEED Gold or equivalent; green building practices; integration with transit to reduce car dependency.

- **Adaptive design**: Facilities that can evolve with changing entertainment and sports trends (e.g., flexible seating, tech infrastructure for emerging uses).

## Fiscal Responsibility

The proposal acknowledges critiques that stadiums rarely produce net new economic benefits and that construction jobs are temporary. It argues that *catalyst* effects—redevelopment of underused land, spin-off development—can justify limited public investment when paired with strong accountability. ROI projections would be subject to independent review before any commitment.

## Trade-offs and Risks

- **Team retention**: Subsidy limits may increase relocation risk; the proposal balances this with incentives tied to long-term commitments.

- **Displacement**: CBAs and anti-displacement funds are proposed but require robust enforcement.

- **Uncertainty**: Economic impact projections are inherently uncertain; the proposal favors conservative assumptions and phased commitments.

## Conclusion

This framework aims to align stadium policy with Chicago's stated criteria: **Impact** (benefits across Chicagoans, accessibility, local engagement), **Fiscal Responsibility** (accountability, sustainability, limited exposure), and **Sustainability** (adaptive design, environmental practices, mixed use). It does not prescribe a specific deal but establishes principles for evaluating future proposals.
